Concept Generation and Concept Selection

At high speed, wheels do not tend to work well as they exhibit wear and heat generation due to friction. To achieve maximum speed, we also want to eliminate friction. Therefore, a low friction suspension system is preferred when traveling at high subsonic speeds. The methods of levitation we looked into for our passenger pod design were air bearings and magnetic levitation.

Air Bearings

The idea of involving air bearings to levitate the passenger pod was proposed in Elon Musk’s “Hyperloop Alpha.”  The main consideration for air bearings was because they have been shown to work at speeds of Mach 1.1 with very low amounts of friction. Similar to the procedure used on hockey table pucks to create a float-like illusion, loads are upheld by thin films of pressurized air. Unlike other types of bearings, air bearings are unique in the sense that they eradicate long-term complications. By significantly reducing friction, problems such as wear and tear and lubricant management become eliminated. The fluid film procedure is composed of a consistent inflow of air being circulated between the bearing face and the bearing gap. The whole circulation process is regulated and controlled by means of an apparatus covered in small openings; this apparatus ensures that a measured amount of air reaches the gap. The methodical release and constraint of the air used in the bearing ensures that the levels of inflow and outflow of air are satisfactory enough to work within the gap. The coordinated discharge of air in the gap is what creates the foundation for the pressure that helps the bearing sustain the working load. In the case of the hyperloop competition, the pod itself would produce an air cushion in order for it to levitate.
In the ground transportation industry, magnetic levitation involves vehicles that are suspended and guided by electromagnetic forces. There are two types of electromagnetic suspension.

Electromagnetic Suspension

Electromagnetic Suspension is based on the attractive force of magnets. There are magnetized coils that run along the track, which are used to repel the magnets under the passenger pod. This allows the pod to levitate above the track that also serves as a guideway. When the train levitates, power is supplied to the coils to produce a network of magnetic fields which moves the passenger pod along the track. The alternating electric current supplied to the coils along the track constantly changes the polarity of the magnetized coils. It is this change in polarity that produces a magnetic field in front of the pod that pulls it forward, while the magnetic field in the back of the pod pushes it forward, producing thrust.

Electrodynamic Suspension

This type of suspension system is based on the repulsive force of magnets. There are superconducting magnets placed on the passenger pod and a track made of a conductive material. The superconducting magnets are the source of the magnetic field, which are at certain height above the conductive track. Levitation is caused by repulsion forces between the electromagnets on the pod and the eddy currents induced into the track as the passenger pod moves along the track. The motion of the magnets cause a change in magnetic field. Varying magnetic fields induce electric currents, called eddy currents, in a conductor, which in this case is the track. The eddy currents then induce a magnetic field in the conductive track. The resulting magnetic field is a similar yet converse to the source magnetic field. These opposing magnetic fields exert a repulsive force which is required for obtaining a lift force. To maintain the repulsive force, the magnet must continuously move over the conductive plate. For instance, a linear synchronous motor or linear induction motor is used for electromagnetic forward motion parallel to the track. However, as the magnet moves along the track, the symmetry of the induced eddy currents are disrupted. This brings about a horizontal component of the magnetic field and a horizontal force on the magnet. According to Lenz’s law, this horizontal force opposes the motion of the magnet. Both lift and drag forces depend on the plate’s conductivity and thickness and on the magnet’s velocity. At high velocities, the lift force converges to a single value, while the drag force is decreases as velocity increases.

Selected Design Solution

The levitating system chosen for our hyperloop pod design was electrodynamic suspension.
Anytime a conductor experiences a changing magnetic field, electric currents are induced, creating a magnetic field to oppose that change. This creates a magnetic field that is nearly a mirror image of the magnetic fields of the magnets, thereby creating a repulsive force. If the change in magnetic field is great enough it will then lead to levitation.
For this project electrodynamic levitation will be performed by using a quadcopter fitted with four spinning halbach magnet arrays. A halbach array is a specific arrangement of magnets used to strengthen the magnetic field of lower portion of the magnetic array while weakening the magnetic field above the magnetic array. Each array consists of sixteen quarter inch cubic grade N52 neodymium magnets that will be placed in a circular arrangement in a 3D printed frame. Permanent magnets were used because they do not require input energy and power for cooling, as opposed to electromagnets; Permanent magnets have a permanent magnetic field. Grade N52 permanent magnets were the strongest permanent magnets available for purchase. The magnetic arrays will be rotated by a brushless AC motor at a speed of 1000 rpm over a sheet of Aluminum 6106 T6. It is the mechanical rotation of these magnetic arrays above the passive conductive rail guideway that will induce an opposing magnetic field into the aluminum sheet. This will result in an opposing magnetic field being created that interacts with the spinning halbach arrays. This opposing field will produce a repulsive force between the conductive material and the magnetic source. The repulsive force between the track and the spinning magnets serves as the lifting force of the pod. The faster the AC motor rotates the magnets, the stronger the magnetic field that will be induced into the track. The magnetic field induced to the track is also expected to increase as the pod accelerates.

Build summary: Selected Design

One of the main obstacles for the hyperloop is using magnetic levitation on top of aluminum. This would limit our design to using electrodynamic suspension for levitation above the track. Another limitation to our design is the limited track length we must operate with, forcing us to use active electrodynamic suspension instead of a passive one. This leaves us with two options, using coils with an alternating current or rotating a circular array of permanent magnets at a very high rpm. The use of coils would be easier to implement except this design option is relatively unstable and has a prohibitively power requirement to match the magnetic field produced by the latter option. Therefore, we chose to use a rotating circular array. The design we came up with is based on the design of a magnetic quadcopter. Using this design would make it easier to have stable levitation without worrying too much about pitch and yaw. To achieve stability the four rotors will be rotating in the direction according to the following figure:

Figure . Direction of spin for each rotor.

This arrangement is used to counteract any moment created by the individual arrays during operation. The next component to consider are the magnets. The magnets that will be used are rare earth N52 cube magnets. These magnets are the strongest magnets available in the market and will be most efficient for inducing a magnetic field into the aluminum track. The magnets will be arranged in a Halbach array to increase the strength of the magnetic field on one side. There will be 16 quarter inch cube magnets per rotor totaling to 64 magnets for the entire levitation system. Each rotor will be attached to an AC brushless motor that will be operating simultaneously. Each rotor will be located at each corner of the frame and will be as close to the frame as possible to mitigate vibrations caused by flexing parts. The motors will be bolted to a cage designed to hold the motor in place relative to the frame. The cage will be attached to the frame from both the top and bottom of the motor.

Linear Induction motor report

The idea of the Linear Induction Motor has floated around since the 1800s, though authors cannot pinpoint the exact time or place where this idea took hold. It is believed that the idea for the linear electric motor is as old as the rotary electric motor. The very first linear motor was made by Charles Wheatstone and it was a reluctance machine in 1845. Nikola Tesla invented the induction motor in 1888. From there, the idea for a linear induction motor got its first patent  in 1895. As time passed, there were more and more breakthroughs in this field. This includes a full-scale aircraft launcher built by Westinghouse in 1946, which was actually an induction motor with a moving primary.

The linear induction motor is a type of linear electric machine that develops electromagnetic forces using the concepts of Faraday’s and Ampere’s laws. These electromagnetic forces combine to produce linear motion. There are two types of linear electric machines, the ones that produce progressive motion and the ones that produce oscillatory motion. 

The shape of machines that produce linear progressive motion are different from the linear oscillatory machines (LOMs).  Progressive motion LEMs are three phase ac devices that tend to operate with brushless configurations. On the other hand, LOMs are typically single-phase ac devices. There are a few advantages to using the progressive motion LEMs, mainly that they can operate at different voltages and frequencies that should change the speed at high efficiency.  This is not the case with LOMs which must operate under a fixed frequency.

The motors that fall under the description of linear progressive motion machines are Linear induction machines, linear synchronous machines, linear brushless dc machines and linear dc brush machines. These were considered when we were deciding what to use to power our miniature hyperloop pod.  

The linear induction motor has been gaining popularity over time. The main application of the LIM and the one thing it is most known for is in transportation, where a part of the motor can be placed on the vehicle and the other part placed on or around the track. LIM are also used in cranes, elevators, actuators that move doors, high voltage circuit breakers and accelerators.According the Korean Society for Railway, they use the LIM as propulsion in their maglev trains because they are lightweight and inexpensive to produce .The most widely used linear motor is the single sided linear induction motor (SLIM). The linear induction motor is a rotary motor that has straightened been out and laid flat on a surface like shown in the figure below.

The primary, which tends to be the stator of the LIM is made up off slot in a rectangular model  that are formed by a stack of steel laminations. In between these slots there are the polyphase windings that produce a magnetic field that is traveling on a linear path, they perform the same function that a rotating magnetic field would have on a rotary induction motor. The secondary of the LIM, which would be the rotor, is usually made of a copper or aluminum sheet and can be accompanied by a solid back iron plate. This is not always the case for the rotor. The main function of this rotor is to complete the magnetic circuit and to create a magnetic flux that links across the air gap between the rotor and the stator. When this link is created, it induces a voltage on the conductive wall which in turn generates an eddy current in the conducting outer layer of the secondary. When the eddy current interacts with the changing electromagnetic field,  electromagnetic thrust is generated on the plate in the linear direction of the motor. To be clear, our design of the linear induction motor will have the primary attached to the pod and it will move along a secondary that will be on the surface of the rail.

Having Three Phase Power in a motor means that there are three alternating currents  uniformly separated in a phase angle. Alternating currents alternate cyclically by flowing in one direction in a circuit and then reversing the flow of the current in the other. When this is happening, the magnitude of the voltage supplied varies continuously between a positive and negative maximum amplitude. As mentioned before, these electric services  are uniformly separated in phase angles. When this occurs the points in time where these legs of alternating current reach the maximum voltage are separated by 1/3 of the time in a full cycle. The constant alternating current from all three of the currents ensures that the total power supplied remains constant. For our project, this is a necessity. The hyperloop cannot suffer drops in power or the magnetic field needed to levitate the pod would vary wildly and not be able to keep a constant level.

In most designs for the three-phase motors, there is one set of windings for each phase. This design has been found to be very efficient and allows for the three phase motor to draw less current than the single – phase motor.

Single phase – motors are not ideal for what we are trying to make. The single phase motor that generates an equivalent amount of horsepower will draw much more current and will also be larger in size. Forces on a linear induction motor

There are three main forces that act on the Linear Induction Motor: normal force, thrust and lateral force. The normal force is perpendicular to the stator in the z-direction. When using a single-sided motor, levitation can be generated from the magnetic field generated during the motors use. In a typical arrangement this magnetic levitation force is negated as an iron backing is used on the secondary, thus the attraction forces overcome the lift generated.  Any forces that move the LIM laterally are undesired and they only develop due to the orientation of the stator.

Forces of a linear induction motor. 

The normal forces behave differently when in an single-sided linear induction motor (SLIM) and when in a double-sided linear induction motor (DLIM) arrangement. A double sided linear induction motor has the reaction plate located between two primary stators. The normal force between the between the stator and the reaction plate is equal and opposite to the second stator. This creates a resultant force that is normal force equal to zero. The only way to see a change in the net normal force of a linear induction motor is to place the reaction plate asymmetrically between the stators. These two forces acting simultaneously on the reaction place allow for the motor to keep the vehicle in line and moving straight as they repel off of the aluminum plate. This was a big draw that made us consider the use of the double sided linear induction motor.

In the case of a single sided linear induction motor, there is no second primary stator to apply an equal and opposite force. Instead, there is a large net normal force between the primary and the secondary because of the fundamental asymmetrical topology. When in a synchronous speed, the force is attractive and the magnitude becomes lessened as the speed dwindles. There are also cases where certain speeds can make the force repulsive.

The main goal of the LIM is develop thrust. The thrust that the LIM generates is proportional to the square of the applied voltage. Thrust is reduced when slip is reduced, and it’s behavior is similar to that of an induction motor with high rotor resistance.

There are also lateral forces that act on the LIM in the y-direction that are perpendicular to the movement of the rotor. These lateral forces have the potential to make the LIM system unstable. To determine the material needed to produce our motor, we looked at similar motors that are already on the market. A typical 3 phase 4 pole linear induction motor, is comprised of a squirrel cage in which the wires are wound around to create the necessary torque. Other motors are also typically made out of iron cores, because of its high permeability. With any device that is required to have an electric current passing through, we also need to worry about magnetic losses due to hysteresis. This occurs due to the magnetic field flowing in all directions as it flows through the core. In order to prevent this and decrease our magnetic losses from the core, a laminated motor is required. This is done by selecting a material and stacking layers of the material with a thickness of approximately .1 to .5 mm, onto one another into the desired shape. This prevents eddy currents from forming within the material, and forcing the current to flow in only one direction, longitudinally in relation to our motor. Looking at transformers, which are devices designed to have high currents and voltages passing through them, as an example for what materials to use, it was determined that steel laminations is the best option to use as a base material for our motor.

Viable Options

During our decision making process, we looked at a few other viable options in which we could look towards using as a means of moving our pod. The linear induction motor, as well as magnetic levitation, were staple components to the design of our pod, thus, we already had a clear goal as to what we needed to design. However, we did analyze a few back ups in case of the event in which we were unable to produce a functioning linear induction motor.

One of the very first options that we discussed using was a standard rotary motor. Rotary motors are simple to come by and could supply the necessary torque that we would need to move our pod. It would not be something that we would need to construct, however finding the right sized motors to supply necessary torque and being able to it within the size constraints of our design may prove to be difficult.

Another option that we decided to look at was constructing a double sided linear induction motor. Instead of having a single motor attached to the bottom of the pod and parallel to the top of the track, we would attach 2 motors to under the pod, which would wrap around the track facing inwards and be perpendicular to the top of the track. This would potentially prove too difficult for various reasons as we would need to make sure our arrangement would be perfect to keep the motors balanced and symmetrical about the track as well as the cost of attaching another motor to the pod would significantly affect our limited budget.

We also considered the use of linear synchronous motors in our design. Linear synchronous motors provide high electrical-to-mechanical efficiency as well as allow for precise control of the position of the vehicle, in addition to allowing for great speeds to be achieved during travel. They however come with the disadvantages of requiring the exact position of the on-board magnets to ensure that the vehicle is synchronous during travel. Use of a LSM also requires the use of an “active track” in which stator coils are placed into the track and are used to energize each block of the track during the vehicles journey.
